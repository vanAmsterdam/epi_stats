Intro

Tutor: Rebecca Stellato

ML vs REML

ML underestimates variances of parameters.

REML is better.

Testing models that differ in correlation part: both ML or REML Testing models that differ in fixed part: only ML is OK

for reporting, do REML

Procedure

  • start with all possible covariates in fixed model
  • experiment with random part to fix correlation structure
  • then reduce fixed part

Testing random effects

variance parameters are strictly >= 0; divide p-value by 2 for LRT chi-square AIC is ok, but don’t use +2 as criterion (is not often used in literature)

Testing fixed effects

use LRT (under ML)

Mixed models work pretty well with missing data on outcome Uses some implicit imputation for missing values.

For missing predictors, imputation will help

Model assumptions

  • linearity of effects (for those you include)
  • normality of residuals
  • normality of intercepts and slopes (however, these are more or less coerced to normal distribution by the random modeling)

Centering covariates

Center ‘time’ axis will change fixed intercept (when there is a fixed effect of time), and SD of random intercept effect (if variation increases with time) and correlation of intercept and slope of random parts

Multilevel models

e.g. longitudinal data from patients of different hospitals

Usually, you want a random intercept on hospital level, due to design, even if it’s not needed by LRT

-> great range of possibilities, think about what makes sense!

Time-varying covariates no problem for mixed models. Gives a single fixed effect.

–> think about case-mix and what to include in hospital level covariates
